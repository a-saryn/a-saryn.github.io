🌿 The Bioscience Problem

In Malaysia, maximizing the yield of Fresh Fruit Bunches (FFB) from oil palms (Elaeis guineensis) is critical for land-use efficiency and economic food security.

As an oil palm plantation matures, its annual yield follows a biological curve: it increases rapidly during early maturity, peaks, and then gradually declines due to aging and canopy shading.

Why Calculus Matters: To ensure food security, agronomists use the first derivative $f'(x)$ to find the precise moment yield growth peaks ($f'(x) = 0$), helping managers time replanting cycles and fertilizer interventions.
Data Reference: Trend modeled based on historical yield curves published in the Malaysian Palm Oil Board (MPOB) annual statistical reports.

📐 Mathematical Modeling

Yield Function $f(x)$
$f(x) = -0.05x^3 + 1.2x^2 - 4x + 5$

Predicts the total annual FFB yield ($t$) in metric tonnes per hectare ($\text{t/ha}$), where $x$ represents the plantation tree age (from $x = 3$ to $22$ years).

Derivative $f'(x)$ (Rate of Change)
$f'(x) = -0.15x^2 + 2.4x - 4$

Calculates the instantaneous growth rate ($\text{t/ha/year}$). Tells us exactly how fast the productivity is increasing or decreasing at any given year.
